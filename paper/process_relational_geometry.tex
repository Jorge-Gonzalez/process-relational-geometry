\title{A Process-Relational Foundation for Geometry}
\author{}
\date{}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We develop a foundational framework for geometry in which primitive relations
and their compositions, rather than a pre-existing spatial substrate, are the
basic data. Finite composable sequences of relations, called configurations,
represent processes. Objects arise as equivalence classes of configurations
under a congruence relation expressing observational indistinguishability.
A quotient construction theorem establishes that these objects inherit a
well-defined associative composition. We further introduce discrete weight
functions and prove a metric emergence result, showing how distance arises
as an optimization over finite relational processes. Concrete discrete
constructions of the circle and torus are provided, along with a relational
account of curvature via noncommutativity. The framework is deliberately
finite and discrete throughout; no appeal to continuity, limits, or infinite
processes is made.
\end{abstract}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}

Classical geometry assumes the prior existence of space as a primitive
entity populated by points. Relations such as distance, adjacency, and
order are then defined on this underlying set. A consequence of this
approach is that metric and positional properties appear to be intrinsic
to geometric objects, when they are in fact dependent on an ambient
reference structure.

We adopt a different starting point. A \emph{process-relational} framework
takes relations and their compositions as the only primitives. No background
space, no points, no coordinates, and no continuous structures are assumed.
Objects emerge as stable invariants under relational indistinguishability,
and any metric or positional property is the result of an explicit
comparison with a chosen reference, not an intrinsic feature of the object
itself.

The framework rests on four commitments:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Relations and their compositions are primitive.
  \item Configurations of relations represent finite processes.
  \item Objects arise as equivalence classes under congruence.
  \item All constructions are finite and discrete.
\end{enumerate}

The last commitment deserves emphasis. Continuity, infinitesimals, and
infinite processes are not part of the framework. This is not a limitation
to be overcome but a foundational choice: geometric structure should be
expressible in terms of finite relational data, or not claimed to exist
within this framework at all.

\section{Relational Systems}

\begin{definition}[Relational System]
A \emph{relational system} is a pair $(R, \circ)$ where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $R$ is a finite or countable set of \emph{primitive relations},
  \item $\circ : R \times R \rightharpoonup R$ is a \emph{partially defined}
        composition operation.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Compatibility]
Two relations $r_1, r_2 \in R$ are \emph{compatible} if $r_1 \circ r_2$
is defined.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Associativity]
A relational system is \emph{associative} if, whenever both sides are
defined,
\[
  (r_1 \circ r_2) \circ r_3 = r_1 \circ (r_2 \circ r_3).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
Partial composition is essential. Requiring total composition would force
all relations to be composable with all others, which is not the case in
general relational structures. Associativity is assumed throughout.
\end{remark}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Configurations as Finite Processes}

\begin{definition}[Configuration]
A \emph{configuration} is a finite composable sequence of relations:
\[
  C = (r_1, r_2, \dots, r_n), \quad r_i \in R,
\]
such that each consecutive pair $r_i, r_{i+1}$ is compatible. The
\emph{empty configuration} $\mathrm{id}$ is included by convention.
\end{definition}

Configurations represent finite processes or paths in the relational
system. Composition of configurations is given by concatenation when the
endpoint of one is compatible with the start of the next. We denote the
set of all configurations by $\mathcal{C}$.

\begin{definition}[Configuration Composition]
For configurations $C = (r_1, \dots, r_m)$ and $D = (s_1, \dots, s_n)$,
their composition is:
\[
  C \circ D = (r_1, \dots, r_m, s_1, \dots, s_n),
\]
defined when $r_m$ and $s_1$ are compatible. The empty configuration acts
as a two-sided identity.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
No limit, no infinite sequence, and no continuity condition appears in
this definition. All configurations are strictly finite objects.
\end{remark}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Congruence and Observational Indistinguishability}

\begin{definition}[Congruence]
An equivalence relation $\sim$ on $\mathcal{C}$ is a \emph{congruence}
with respect to composition if, for all configurations
$C_1, C_1', C_2, C_2'$,
\[
  C_1 \sim C_1' \;\text{ and }\; C_2 \sim C_2'
  \;\Rightarrow\;
  C_1 \circ C_2 \sim C_1' \circ C_2',
\]
whenever the compositions are defined.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Observational Indistinguishability]
\label{def:obs-indist}
Configurations $C_1$ and $C_2$ are \emph{observationally indistinguishable}
with respect to a congruence $\sim$ if, for every compatible configuration
$D$,
\[
  C_1 \circ D \sim C_2 \circ D
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  D \circ C_1 \sim D \circ C_2,
\]
whenever the respective compositions are defined.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
Definition~\ref{def:obs-indist} specifies what it means for two
configurations to be \emph{equivalent within} a given congruence $\sim$:
no compositional test with any other configuration can distinguish them.
This is a property of a pair $(C_1, C_2)$ relative to an already-given
congruence. It is not a definition of $\sim$ itself, which avoids any
circularity. In practice, one first specifies $\sim$ by the relational
laws of the system, then verifies that it satisfies the congruence property
and that equivalent configurations are observationally indistinguishable.
\end{remark}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Objects and the Quotient Construction}

\begin{definition}[Object]
An \emph{object} is an equivalence class $[C]$ of a configuration $C$
under a congruence $\sim$:
\[
  [C] = \{ C' \in \mathcal{C} \mid C' \sim C \}.
\]
\end{definition}

Objects represent stable relational behaviors: configurations that no
compositional test can distinguish are identified as the same object.

\begin{theorem}[Quotient Construction]
\label{thm:quotient}
Let $(R, \circ)$ be an associative relational system with configuration
set $\mathcal{C}$. If $\sim$ is a congruence on $\mathcal{C}$, then:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item The quotient $\mathcal{C}/{\sim}$ is well-defined as a set of
        equivalence classes.
  \item Composition descends to a well-defined operation on
        $\mathcal{C}/{\sim}$:
        \[
          [C_1] \circ [C_2] := [C_1 \circ C_2].
        \]
  \item The induced composition on $\mathcal{C}/{\sim}$ is associative.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{(i)} By definition, $\sim$ partitions $\mathcal{C}$ into disjoint
nonempty equivalence classes. The quotient is the set of these classes.

\textbf{(ii)} We must show independence of representatives. Suppose
$[C_1] = [C_1']$ and $[C_2] = [C_2']$, so $C_1 \sim C_1'$ and
$C_2 \sim C_2'$. Since $\sim$ is a congruence,
\[
  C_1 \circ C_2 \sim C_1' \circ C_2',
\]
hence $[C_1 \circ C_2] = [C_1' \circ C_2']$. The operation is therefore
independent of the choice of representatives.

\textbf{(iii)} For any $C_1, C_2, C_3 \in \mathcal{C}$, associativity
at the configuration level gives
$(C_1 \circ C_2) \circ C_3 = C_1 \circ (C_2 \circ C_3)$.
Passing to equivalence classes and applying (ii):
\[
  ([C_1] \circ [C_2]) \circ [C_3]
  = [(C_1 \circ C_2) \circ C_3]
  = [C_1 \circ (C_2 \circ C_3)]
  = [C_1] \circ ([C_2] \circ [C_3]). \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The quotient construction does not introduce new structure. It stabilizes
invariants already present in the relational system by identifying
configurations that behave identically under all compositions.
Geometric structure emerges from the relational data, not from the act
of quotienting.
\end{remark}

\section{Discrete Metric Emergence}

We now show how a notion of distance can arise from the relational system
without being imposed as a primitive. The construction is entirely
discrete and finite.

\begin{definition}[Discrete Weight Function]
A \emph{discrete weight function} is a map $w : R \to \mathbb{N}$
extended to configurations by:
\[
  w(C) = w(r_1) + w(r_2) + \cdots + w(r_n)
  \quad\text{for } C = (r_1, \dots, r_n),
  \quad w(\mathrm{id}) = 0.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Relational Path]
A \emph{relational path from $[A]$ to $[B]$} is a configuration $P$
such that $P$ composed from a representative of $[A]$ reaches a
representative of $[B]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Discrete Metric Emergence]
\label{thm:metric}
Let $w$ be a discrete weight function on a relational system $(R, \circ)$
with congruence $\sim$. Assume:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(A\arabic*)]
  \item $w(r) \geq 0$ for all $r \in R$,
  \item $w(C_1) = w(C_2)$ whenever $C_1 \sim C_2$,
  \item $w(\mathrm{id}) = 0$.
\end{enumerate}
Define:
\[
  d([A],[B]) = \min\bigl\{ w(P) \mid P \text{ is a relational path from }
  [A] \text{ to } [B] \bigr\}.
\]
Then $d$ is a pseudometric on $\mathcal{C}/{\sim}$. If additionally
$d([A],[B]) = 0 \Rightarrow [A] = [B]$, then $d$ is a metric.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Non-negativity.} $d([A],[B]) \geq 0$ follows from (A1) and
the definition as a minimum over non-negative values.

\textbf{Symmetry.} If the relational system contains inverse relations
(i.e., for every $r$ there exists $r^{-1}$ with $w(r^{-1}) = w(r)$),
then reversing a path from $[A]$ to $[B]$ yields a path of equal weight
from $[B]$ to $[A]$, giving $d([A],[B]) = d([B],[A])$.

\textbf{Triangle inequality.} Given paths $P$ from $[A]$ to $[B]$ and
$Q$ from $[B]$ to $[C]$, the concatenation $P \circ Q$ is a path from
$[A]$ to $[C]$ with $w(P \circ Q) = w(P) + w(Q)$. Taking minima:
\[
  d([A],[C]) \leq w(P \circ Q) = w(P) + w(Q).
\]
Minimizing over all such $P$ and $Q$ gives
$d([A],[C]) \leq d([A],[B]) + d([B],[C])$.

The separation condition $d([A],[B]) = 0 \Rightarrow [A] = [B]$ upgrades
the pseudometric to a metric by (A2) and (A3).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The use of $\min$ rather than $\inf$ is deliberate and reflects the
discrete, finite character of the framework. In a finite relational
system, the minimum over all paths of bounded weight is always attained.
No limiting process is required.
\end{remark}

\section{Examples}

\subsection{Discrete Circle}

Let $R = \{a\}$ with total composition $a \circ a = a^2$, and so on.
Configurations are:
\[
  \mathrm{id},\; a,\; a^2,\; \dots,\; a^k,\; \dots
\]
with composition $a^k \circ a^\ell = a^{k+\ell}$.

Fix $n \geq 1$. Define a congruence by:
\[
  a^k \sim a^m \;\iff\; k \equiv m \pmod{n}.
\]
This is a congruence because $k \equiv k' \pmod{n}$ and $\ell \equiv \ell'
\pmod{n}$ imply $k + \ell \equiv k' + \ell' \pmod{n}$.

By Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient}, the quotient is:
\[
  \mathcal{C}/{\sim} \;=\; \{[a^0],[a^1],\dots,[a^{n-1}]\}
  \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z},
\]
with composition $[a^k] \circ [a^\ell] = [a^{k+\ell \bmod n}]$.

This cyclic structure is the \emph{discrete circle of resolution $n$}.
The geometric content of the circle --- closure, periodicity, uniform
step structure --- is encoded entirely in the single relational law
$a^n \sim \mathrm{id}$. No embedding space, no coordinates, and no
continuous arc are used or needed.

\begin{remark}
The parameter $n$ is not an intrinsic property of the circle. It
represents the resolution at which the circle is observed relative to
the step size of the relational system. Different values of $n$ are
the same relational structure seen from different comparative contexts,
not different geometric objects.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Discrete Torus}

Let $R = \{a, b\}$. Impose the congruence generated by:
\[
  a \circ b \sim b \circ a, \quad a^n \sim \mathrm{id}, \quad b^m \sim \mathrm{id}.
\]
The first law encodes commutativity of the two generators. The second and
third encode independent periodicities.

By Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient}:
\[
  \mathcal{C}/{\sim} \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \;\times\; \mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}.
\]
This is the \emph{discrete torus of resolution $(n,m)$}, constructed
from two independent periodic relational directions without any reference
to a background plane or embedding.

\subsection{Relational Curvature via Noncommutativity}

Let $R = \{a, b, a^{-1}, b^{-1}\}$ with the usual cancellation laws
$a \circ a^{-1} \sim \mathrm{id}$ and $b \circ b^{-1} \sim \mathrm{id}$.
If $a \circ b \not\sim b \circ a$, define the \emph{commutator}:
\[
  [a,b] = a \circ b \circ a^{-1} \circ b^{-1}.
\]
A nontrivial commutator --- one where $[a,b] \not\sim \mathrm{id}$ ---
encodes the failure of the two relational directions to close back to
their starting point. This is the discrete relational analogue of
holonomy: the observable record of traveling around a closed path and
returning to a different relational state.

Curvature in this framework is the degree to which relational composition
fails to commute. It is a discrete, computable property of the relational
system, not a differential invariant.

\section{Categorical Interpretation}

The construction of Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient} has a natural categorical
reading.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Primitive relations in $R$ are \emph{generating morphisms}.
  \item Configurations in $\mathcal{C}$ are \emph{composable chains} of
        morphisms, forming a free category on $R$.
  \item The congruence $\sim$ \emph{imposes identifications}, quotienting
        the free category by the relational laws.
  \item The quotient $\mathcal{C}/{\sim}$ is the \emph{category presented
        by generators $R$ and relations ${\sim}$}.
\end{itemize}

When the relational laws force every generator to have a compositional
inverse up to $\sim$, the quotient is a \emph{groupoid}. The discrete
circle construction yields the cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$
viewed as a one-object groupoid.

\begin{remark}
The framework builds structures analogous to those arising in
\emph{string rewriting systems} and \emph{presentations of monoids and
categories}. The geometric content is carried by the choice of generators
and relations, not by any ambient space.
\end{remark}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Higher Relational Structure}

The framework extends to higher levels by allowing equivalences between
configurations to themselves carry relational structure.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Primitive relations in $R$ are \emph{$0$-cells}.
  \item Configurations (composable chains) are \emph{$1$-cells} or
        \emph{$1$-morphisms}.
  \item Congruences between configurations are \emph{$2$-morphisms}.
  \item Congruences between congruences are \emph{$3$-morphisms}, and
        so on.
\end{itemize}

This hierarchy corresponds to the structure of a \emph{strict
$\omega$-category} or, when all morphisms at each level are invertible,
a \emph{strict $\omega$-groupoid}. The discrete circle at this level of
generality is the fundamental groupoid on a finite graph, with paths
as $1$-morphisms and homotopies between paths as $2$-morphisms.

All levels of this hierarchy remain finite and discrete if the generating
data at each level is finite.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Connections to Existing Frameworks}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Category theory.} The quotient construction is an
        instance of forming a category by generators and relations.
        The work of Mac Lane~\cite{maclane} and Awodey~\cite{awodey}
        provides the general categorical background.

  \item \textbf{String rewriting and monoid presentations.} The
        relational system $(R, \sim)$ is a string rewriting system;
        the quotient is the monoid or category it presents. See
        Book and Otto~\cite{bookotto} for the general theory.

  \item \textbf{Algebraic topology.} The discrete analogues of paths,
        homotopies, and fundamental groupoids connect to combinatorial
        algebraic topology. Hatcher~\cite{hatcher} provides classical
        background; the discrete perspective is developed
        in~\cite{may}.

  \item \textbf{Discrete geometry.} The framework is consistent with
        approaches that treat geometric structure as fundamentally
        discrete and finite, as in graph-based models of
        space~\cite{graphgeom}.
\end{itemize}

Unlike classical approaches in all of these areas, the present framework
does not assume a background space, does not invoke continuity or limits,
and treats all constructions as intrinsically finite.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}

We have shown that discrete geometric and algebraic structures can be
derived from primitive finite relations and their composition through
congruence and quotient constructions. The central results are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient}}: A congruence on a
        relational system yields a well-defined quotient with associative
        composition.
  \item \textbf{Theorem~\ref{thm:metric}}: A discrete weight function
        yields a pseudometric (or metric) on the quotient, derived from
        path optimization over finite relational data.
\end{itemize}

Concrete constructions of the discrete circle, discrete torus, and
relational curvature demonstrate the framework's expressive range within
a strictly finite setting.

Directions for further development include:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Classification of congruences on a given relational system and
        the geometric structures they produce.
  \item Reconstruction of classical discrete spaces (graphs, simplicial
        complexes, finite topological spaces) within the framework.
  \item Formalization in the language of higher category theory,
        keeping all structures finite.
  \item Characterization of which discrete relational structures
        are observationally equivalent, i.e., produce isomorphic
        quotients.
\end{enumerate}
